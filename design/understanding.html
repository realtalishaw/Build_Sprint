0. How Might We
HMW's & Affinity Mapping

The ‚ÄúHow Might We‚Äù or HMW method is an essential Design Sprint methodology. At Google, we use it to capture opportunities during lightning talks and throughout the Understand phase. This method allows your team to take the insights and pain points they hear and positively reframe them. HMWs create an active framework for resolving the challenges. It‚Äôs important to explain to your team how the HMW process works before Lightning Talks begin. Make sure the team understands they are looking for opportunities rather than solutions. Each word of the HMW phrase puts team members in the right mindset:

  • ‚ÄúHow‚Äù guides team members to believe the answer is out there.
  • ‚ÄúMight‚Äù lets team members know their HMW statement might or might not work, and either possibility is okay
  • ‚ÄúWe‚Äù reminds team members that the Design Sprint is about teamwork and building on each others‚Äô ideas

Do not spend a lot of time crafting or perfecting HMW statements. Instead, encourage your team to come up with as many HMW notes as they can, with as little self-editing as possible. This is an exercise in expansive thinking aimed at preventing the team from arriving at premature solutions.

Directions:

  • Set a timer for 30 minutes
  • Use a thick market to write your HMW notes
  • When you hear pain points, regrame them as opportunities
  • Write only one HMW idea or opportunity per sticky note
  • Aim for quanity over perfection
  • Group similar ideas together

1. User Interviews
Getting Feedback from real users

Some Design Sprints may include user interviews in the Understand phase to inject first-hand user perspectives into the Sprint. User interviews rely on stories to explore emotions,understand user goals, and assess needs. The goal of the interview is to gain empathy for the user‚Äôs experience. Through that empathy, we can shape the design solutions to fit the use better.

For example, if you are working on a new project on digital watches, you might want to know how people currently keep track of time. What do they use? What do they like? What is a pain point?

Two other, similar interview types to consider for your Design Sprint are Expert Interviews and Stakeholder Interviews..

Directions

  • Recruit users and prepare interview questions
  • Craft a strong introduction for the user to clarify the context of the interview
  • Concentrate on the user's experience with the problem
  • Ask about their likes and dislikes
  • Ask questions that will elicit user stores rather than yes or no responses
  • Make sure 2/3 of the interview is devoted to the user talking and you listening
  • Gather and quanitate all user feedback

2. User Journey Mapping
Map out the Customer Journey step by step

User Journey Mapping is a common Design Sprint method that maps out a user‚Äôs experience step by step as they encounter your problem space or interact with your product. This method enables the team to get into the mindset of the user and illuminates pain points, identifying opportunities to create new or improved user experiences.

Journey maps are often for a specific type of user, also known as a persona. If there many users or players involved in the Design Sprint‚Äôs problem space, multiple journey maps may be required, one for each user type.

How to start a journey map depends on where you are in your product cycle. If you‚Äôre doing a Design Sprint for a new product and/or you are in the early stages of the product cycle, you may want to explore a certain use case for your product and start your journey map with the user‚Äôs initial entry point into that use case. If you have an existing product and are further along in the cycle, you may start your journey map when the user is first introduced to your product, when they‚Äôre searching for your product, or when they are onboarding and/or setting up an account.

Directions

  • Start with the user's first step or entry point into your prooduct experience
  • Add each step in the journey until the user;s goal has been reached
  • Include descriptions for each step and highlight pain points along the journey

3. Experience Mapping
Using the user's journey to map their experience

Experience Mapping is a common UX method where as a team you map out step by step the experience a specific user has within a problem space or context. This is slightly different then a Customer Journey Map. It is a helpful exercise to get the entire team to build empathy with the user‚Äôs motivations, needs and pain points.

An Experience Map is helpful if you are developing a new product, or if you have an existing product that you want to look for opportunities to improve. The important thing is that you stepping above your product flow and looking at the larger context a user is operating in.

Directions

  • Start with the first moment the user has a need for example "I'd like to get in shape" or "I think I might want to make a job change"
  • Then list out each step that they go through to try and fill their need currently
  • Include descriptions for each step and highlight pain points
  • You can also add in layers for platforms (Mobile, Desktop) or environmental factors(In the car, at home)

4. Empathy Building Exercises
Using Empathy when thinking about desiugn

Empathy Building Exercises are a Design Sprint method to get the team further into the user‚Äôs mindset by using the product on which the Design Sprint focuses. This helps the team develop ideas that better meet the user‚Äôs needs

For example, if the Design Sprint is for a mobile app, the team might use the app to complete a specific task. Then, the team reflects on their experience in writing, paying particular attention to any pain points encountered, as well as any moments of delight.

Directions

  • Choose a critical task for your product
  • Set aside 30 minutes
  • Complete the task and record observations

5. Comparable Problem
Collect Examples to prepare for sketch phase

The Comparable Problem method is a great warm up for the Sketch phase of the Design Sprint. Sometimes the best ideas already exist‚Äîthey simply need to be repurposed, presented in a new context, or combined with other ideas. Ask team members to research industries related or in parallel to your project, and to review similar business problems and their solutions. This can be assigned as homework at the end of the Understand phase or completed during the Sprint.

An example exercise: If your product is coffee, look at how other companies have described or categorized their product offering for chocolate.

Directions

  • Look for ideas from other industries or fields that relate toy your business problem
